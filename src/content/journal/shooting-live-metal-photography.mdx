Concert photography is a discipline built around constraints. At most shows you get three songs — sometimes fewer. The lighting is unpredictable, the movement is fast, and the pit can be cramped and chaotic. Metal shows push all of those factors to the extreme.

Here's what I've taken away from shooting in that environment.

## Exposure is a constant negotiation

There's no single setting that works for a full show. Venue lighting shifts between songs, between sections of the stage, and sometimes mid-riff. I shoot in manual and adjust constantly — but I've learned to lock in a base exposure for the dominant light and make small corrections rather than chasing every change.

For darker venues, I'll typically start at ISO 3200–6400, 1/400s, and open the aperture as wide as it's useful. Sharpness matters more than noise.

## The three-song rule changes how you shoot

Knowing you have a hard stop reframes everything. The first song is for getting oriented — reading the stage, watching how the band moves, finding the angles. By the second song you should be working with intent. The third is for anything you haven't gotten yet.

Don't waste the first song hunting for the perfect frame. Shoot to understand the show first.

## Light is the subject

Metal lighting design tends toward extremes — deep shadows, hard rim lighting, strobes timed to the kick drum. The worst version of this kind of photography treats light as an obstacle. The better version treats it as the material you're working with.

Some of my favorite shots from shows are backlit silhouettes, strobed motion, or a single spotlight isolating a face in otherwise complete darkness. You have to be willing to let most of the frame go black.

## Gear matters less than positioning

I shoot with a 24–70mm f/2.8 for most shows. It's versatile enough to cover the full stage width and get close on faces without needing to switch lenses in a moving pit. I'll bring a 70–200mm for larger venues where I'm further back.

But positioning beats focal length every time. Getting to the front barrier early, understanding where the best light hits the stage, knowing which side the vocalist favors — those decisions make more difference than anything in your bag.

## Edit for atmosphere, not accuracy

A metal show doesn't look like natural light. The images shouldn't either. I push contrast hard, let shadows clip, and lean into the color of the stage lighting rather than correcting it to neutral. The goal is to make someone feel like they were there — not to document what the room technically looked like.
